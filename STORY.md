# STORY

## Logline

An elderly woman knitting beneath her soldier husband's portrait is pulled, through memory and image, into the battlefield that took him, only for that war to re-emerge in the present and destroy her home from the sky.

## Synopsis

In a quiet field, a lone rural house stands untouched. Inside, an elderly woman in traditional folk clothing knits in silence. Behind her hangs a portrait of her husband as a young soldier. The camera drifts toward the portrait until it becomes the world itself: first a frozen black-and-white memory, then a living battlefield as the husband runs through smoke and explosion. At the moment war becomes fully alive, the image turns to color. The camera rushes into the side of a tank, travels through its steel body, and exits through the barrel into a contemporary FPV flight path. The drone races low over the same field toward the same house from the opening. The house explodes. Home and battlefield are revealed to have never been separate.

## Beginning

The film establishes domestic peace, age, ritual, and memory. The woman is not active in plot terms; her stillness gives the camera permission to become the storyteller. The portrait is introduced as a relic, then becomes a portal.

## Middle

The husband's memory is no longer static. He is alive, running through battlefield chaos. The shift from black and white to color marks memory becoming immediate and war becoming present-tense. The tank transition bridges old mechanized warfare to its modern airborne descendant.

## End

The FPV flight transforms the audience from observer into weapon trajectory. The house from the first image returns, now vulnerable. Its destruction is the final statement: war that once belonged to the portrait has crossed the threshold into the home.

## Emotional Arc

Calm -> reverence -> unease -> panic -> inevitability -> shock.

## Themes

- War is inherited, not finished.
- Domestic life is fragile against historical violence.
- Memory is not passive; it can reopen the present.
- Technology changes, but the target remains human shelter and family life.

## Scene Goals

### Scene 1: House and Woman

Establish peace, age, cultural rootedness, and the physical house that must be remembered for the ending.

### Scene 2: Portrait to Battlefield

Make the husband legible as both beloved memory and active soldier. Turn archival distance into lived danger.

### Scene 3: Tank to FPV

Create the conceptual bridge between past war machinery and contemporary remote violence without dialogue.

### Scene 4: Strike

Return to the opening house and destroy it in a way that feels tragic, not sensational.
